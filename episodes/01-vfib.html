Cardiac physiology

The conduction system

The SA node fires spontaneously 60–100 times per minute. Each impulse spreads through the atria, reaches the AV node — which delays it briefly — then travels down the bundle of His, left and right bundle branches, and Purkinje fibers, causing coordinated ventricular contraction.

Ventricular fibrillation

Chaotic, disorganized electrical activity. No coordinated contraction. No cardiac output. No pulse. Consciousness lost within 10 seconds. V-fib does not self-terminate — it degrades to asystole within 4–6 minutes without defibrillation. Every minute without a shock reduces survival by ~10%.

Pulseless electrical activity (PEA)

Organized electrical activity on the monitor with no palpable pulse. Non-shockable. The heart is firing electrically but not contracting effectively. Almost always caused by a reversible underlying problem — finding and fixing that cause is the entire treatment.

Asystole

No electrical activity. Flatline. Non-shockable. Almost no survivors outside hospital. V-fib that goes untreated long enough degrades into asystole — which is why immediate defibrillation in V-fib is so critical.

ACLS protocol — V-fib / pulseless V-tach

01Confirm cardiac arrest — unresponsive, no normal breathing, no pulse. Call for help. Start CPR immediately.
02Defibrillate as soon as the device is available — 200J biphasic. Call clear. Confirm no contact. Shock.
03Resume CPR immediately after the shock — 2 full minutes. Rate 100–120/min, depth ≥2 inches, full recoil, minimize interruptions.
04Establish IV or IO access and push epinephrine 1mg IV. Repeat every 3–5 minutes throughout the arrest.
05Rhythm check at 2 minutes — if still V-fib, shock again at 200J and resume CPR immediately.
06After 2nd or 3rd failed shock: push amiodarone 300mg IV (or lidocaine 1–1.5mg/kg if amiodarone unavailable) during the CPR cycle.
07Search for and treat reversible causes simultaneously — the Hs & Ts. Do not stop CPR to do this.

Key drugs

Epinephrine1mg IV/IO q3–5min
Vasoconstriction → increases coronary and cerebral perfusion pressure. Does not convert rhythm but improves ROSC rates. Give as soon as IV access established.
Amiodarone300mg IV, then 150mg
Antiarrhythmic for shock-refractory V-fib after 2–3 shocks. Blocks Na, K, Ca channels. Given during a CPR cycle, not instead of a shock.
Lidocaine1–1.5mg/kg IV
Alternative antiarrhythmic if amiodarone unavailable or contraindicated. Similar efficacy in shock-refractory V-fib.
Calcium gluconateempirical in hyperK
Membrane stabilizer in hyperkalemia. Reasonable to give empirically in refractory V-fib if hyperkalemia suspected — ischemia releases intracellular potassium.
Sodium bicarbonatenot routine
Only if known severe acidosis or hyperkalemia. Blind bicarbonate can worsen intracellular acidosis and cause hypernatremia. Not routine in cardiac arrest.
Norepinephrinepost-ROSC vasopressor
First-line vasopressor for post-arrest hypotension. Target MAP ≥65 mmHg. Stronger evidence than dopamine in post-arrest setting.

Post-ROSC care bundle

Airway

Intubate if GCS <8 or patient cannot protect airway. Target SpO2 94–98% — avoid hyperoxia. Avoid hyperventilation — causes cerebral vasoconstriction.

Circulation

Target MAP ≥65 mmHg. Start norepinephrine if hypotensive. Post-arrest myocardial dysfunction is common — fluids alone are usually insufficient.

Neurology

Targeted temperature management (TTM) — 33–36°C for 24 hours in comatose survivors. Strongest intervention for neurological outcome. Do not prognose before 72h post-arrest.

Coronary

Get a 12-lead ECG immediately. ST-elevation = emergent cath lab activation regardless of neurological status. Do not delay PCI for unconscious state.

Glucose

Target glucose 140–180 mg/dL. Tight control (80–110) increases mortality per NICE-SUGAR trial. Post-arrest hyperglycemia independently worsens neurological outcomes.

Avoid

Fever (>37.5°C), hyperoxia, hyperventilation, tight glycemic control, and premature neurological prognostication — all worsen outcomes.
